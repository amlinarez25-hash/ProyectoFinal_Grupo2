What is Matcha?


Matcha is a finely ground green tea that originates from Japan. It is known for its high content of antioxidants and nutrients. Unlike other teas, where the leaves are steeped, matcha allows you to obtain greater benefits.

It has become popular worldwide for its energizing effects, its ability to improve concentration, and its uses in food and various desserts.

Historically, matcha has deep cultural roots in Japan, where it has been used for centuries in traditional tea ceremonies. Introduced from China during the 12th century, it was embraced by Zen Buddhist monks who valued it for its calming yet stimulating properties during meditation. Over time, matcha became a symbol of mindfulness, respect, and harmony in Japanese culture, and these values continue to influence the way it is prepared and enjoyed today.

Benefits

of Matcha



Matcha provides benefits that balance body and mind, from natural energy
to antioxidants that support your well-being.



Sustained increase of energy and concentration from caffeine molecules and amino acids that together promote relaxation.



It has a powerful antioxidant action that protects against cellular damage and premature aging.




It has a fat-burning effect, supporting cardiovascular and digestive health.



It may improve skin health, strengthen the immune system, and help regulate sugar and cholesterol levels.


How to prepare
Matcha


Preparing matcha is a simple ritual that transforms green powder into a smooth, frothy, and vibrant drink.

1.

Prepare the matcha paste:
Place the matcha powder in the bowl. Pour in a small amount of hot water (approximately 50–60 °C) to make a thick, lump-free paste.


2.

Whisk with the bamboo whisk:
Use the bamboo whisk to gently mix the paste at the bottom of the bowl until no lumps remain.



3.

Add more water:
Pour the remaining hot water into the mixture and continue whisking with quick “W” or “M” motions until a thin layer of foam forms on the surface.



4.

All done!
Your matcha tea is ready to drink.


What is Taro?


Taro is a starchy root vegetable that has been cultivated for thousands of years and is considered one of the oldest crops
in human history. Known scientifically as Colocasia esculenta, it is native to Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands, where it
plays a central role in traditional diets.

Its mildly sweet and nutty flavor makes it versatile in both savory and sweet dishes, from stews and soups to desserts and bubble tea.

Beyond its cultural importance, taro is valued for its nutritional benefits. It is rich in dietary fiber, complex carbohydrates, and essential minerals such as potassium and magnesium. In recent years, taro has gained worldwide recognition, especially in modern cafés and dessert shops, where it is celebrated for both its unique flavor and its natural purple hue that makes drinks and treats visually appealing.

Benefits

of Taro



Taro is valued for its nutritional benefits. It is rich in dietary fiber, which supports digestion.
It contains complex carbohydrates that provide sustained energy.



Taro is high in dietary fiber, which helps keep your digestive system running smoothly and prevents constipation.


Its rich potassium content helps regulate blood pressure and promotes a healthy cardiovascular system.





Packed with vitamin C and antioxidants, taro strengthens the immune system and protects the body against illness.



The complex carbohydrates in taro release energy slowly, keeping you full and energized for longer periods.


How to prepare
Taro


Preparing Taro is a simple ritual that transforms Taro root into a smooth, frothy, and earthy drink.

1.

Cook the taro root:
Peel and cut taro into chunks, then boil until soft. Mash or blend into a smooth, creamy paste.


2.

Prepare the base:
In a blender, combine the taro paste with milk (dairy or plant-based) until it’s thick and silky.



3.

Add flavor:
Stir in sugar, honey, or syrup to taste. A drop of vanilla or a pinch of cinnamon can be added for a unique twist. This balance of flavors brings out taro’s natural nutty and subtly sweet taste.



4.

All done!
Pour the taro mix over brewed black tea or enjoy it on its own.


What is Chai?


Chai is a traditional Indian tea made by brewing black tea with a mix of aromatic spices, herbs, milk, and sweetener. The word chai simply means “tea” in Hindi, but what many know as “chai” or “chai tea” is actually masala chai, which translates to “spiced tea.” Common spices include cardamom, cinnamon, ginger, cloves, and black pepper, creating a warm, fragrant, and slightly sweet flavor.

Today, chai is enjoyed worldwide both as a comforting homemade drink and as a café favorite like the chai latte.

Beyond its popularity as a flavorful beverage, chai holds deep cultural significance in India, where it is often shared as a gesture of hospitality and community. Its blend of spices is not only pleasing to the senses but also offers various health benefits, from aiding digestion to boosting immunity. This balance of tradition, wellness, and taste has helped chai become a global symbol of warmth and connection.

Benefits

of Chai



Chai offers benefits that nurture body and mind, from gentle energy to spices rich in antioxidants
that support your well-being.



Made with black tea, chai provides a natural caffeine kick that helps improve focus and alertness.


Spices like ginger, cardamom, and cloves aid in digestion and can help reduce bloating.



Cinnamon and ginger are rich in antioxidants and have anti-inflammatory properties that support the immune system.

It may improve skin health, strengthen the immune system, and help regulate sugar and cholesterol levels.


How to prepare
Chai


Preparing chai is a soothing ritual that blends black tea and spices into a warm, aromatic, and energizing drink.

1.

Boil water and spices:
Bring water to a boil and add spices like cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, and ginger. Let them simmer to release their flavors.


2.

Add tea leaves:
Stir in black tea leaves and let them steep until strong and aromatic.



3.

Mix in milk and sweetener:
Pour in milk (dairy or plant-based) and add sugar or honey to taste. Simmer for a few more minutes.



4.

All done!
Strain the tea into cups and enjoy hot, fragrant chai.
